# C13-005: Describe Observable Behavior Instead of Internal Emotion

**Method:** YY Method™ Home Edition v2.3 — Capture → Why → Why-Not → Commit → Timestamp

**Status:** Decided

**Date:** 2026-07-24

**Domain:** Character Direction

**Depends On:** C13-004

**Freshness Boundary:** Active. Revisit if future systems consistently infer nuanced performance from abstract emotional labels alone.

**Source:** Public-safe synthesis from iterative AI video production. Exact prompts, generated clips, reference libraries, and platform-specific production fixtures remain private.

---

## Capture

Characters were initially directed with labels such as confused, relieved, worried, proud, or determined.

The same word could produce theatrical exaggeration, a nearly blank expression, or body language that contradicted the face. Small wording changes created entirely different performances.

---

## Why

Emotional language is efficient among humans.

An actor understands that confusion may involve hesitation, searching eyes, a narrowed brow, and a pause before speaking. The word acts as shorthand for embodied knowledge.

---

## Why-Not

The generator was not experiencing the internal state and translating it into behavior. It was sampling visible associations with the label.

Observable actions were more stable: study the bridge, tilt the head, narrow the eyes, pause, glance toward the student.

The scar was recognizing that the camera never records confusion. It records behavior from which the audience infers confusion.

---

## Commit

**Decision:** Direct performance through camera-visible behavior first.

Describe gaze, posture, timing, facial movement, and interaction. Use an emotional label only as secondary context when the observable behavior already carries the intended meaning.

Once the featured subject becomes more controllable, the uncontrolled behavior of everyone else becomes conspicuous.
