## Capture

In the current year, the operator won the event by a significant margin. The result was achieved without deploying all available optimization mechanisms — the highest-ceiling plays were not required to produce a decisive outcome. The 9x expected-value engine alone was sufficient.

This outcome reveals a structural property of the event that was not visible before: the scoring system, when understood at the optimization level, yields near-deterministic dominance. A participant who has solved the scoring geometry, prepared candidate words by theme and tile value, and applies a pre-committed round structure will consistently produce scores at the upper range of what the format permits under proper rules.

The constraint is not vocabulary or time or theme — it is the presence of a participant who has actually analyzed the system. In the absence of another participant who has done the same analysis, the outcome is not genuinely uncertain.

---

## Why

This property was hidden during the preparation phase because the prior year's result suggested another participant had achieved comparably high scores. Once the prior anomaly is explained (C8-030), the competition baseline resolves to: unprepared participants competing against a participant with full optimization knowledge. That comparison is not a contest.

The solvability finding does not reflect poorly on the other participants. Most are attending a charitable, social event. They are not there to solve a scoring optimization problem. The point is not that they are weak competitors — it is that the event format does not require optimization to participate enjoyably. The operator is the only participant applying the optimization frame.

This distinction is important: the event is not broken. It is simply not designed to resist a fully optimized participant.

---

## Why-Not

**Why not conclude that the system is fully solved and the preparation is complete?**
Near-deterministic dominance in the current competition baseline does not mean the system is solved in all configurations. A different baseline — other prepared participants, a modified format, or team play with a junior partner — could reintroduce genuine uncertainty. The finding applies to the current state of the competition, not to all possible states.

**Why not conclude that the preparation was excessive?**
The preparation was proportionate to the operator's goal: to win clean, under proper rules, with a verifiable board. The solvability finding is a consequence of the event format's structure, not of over-preparation. The preparation that produced a margin-winning result under proper rules is exactly what was intended. The issue is not the preparation — it is the structural mismatch between an optimizing participant and a non-optimizing field.

---

## Commit

**Decision:** The event, in its current format, has a near-deterministic ceiling when an optimizing participant is present. The current competition baseline does not include other optimizing participants. The outcome is not genuinely uncertain when the operator participates at full capability. This is a structural property of the system, now confirmed by result.

**Confidence:** High. The empirical result is consistent with the analysis.
